# CLIL/EMI Evidence Base — Verified Research

## What Does a 30% English Lesson Actually Look Like?

"30%" refers to the proportion of lesson time where English is the functional language — including teacher talk, materials, and student output. **No published research prescribes 30% as a validated threshold.** It's a practical implementation target.

### Minute-by-Minute Example: Grade 7 Math (45 min, ~30% English)

| Time | Activity | Language | Rationale |
|------|----------|----------|-----------|
| 0-3 | Vocabulary pre-teach: "equation," "variable," "solve," "both sides" | **EN** | Students need to hear terms multiple times |
| 3-15 | Direct instruction: concept of balancing equations | VN | Core concept in strongest language |
| 15-20 | Guided practice: teacher models using English math language | **EN** | Procedural language in context |
| 20-32 | Student practice: bilingual worksheets, pair work | VN + EN terms | English terms within VN discussion |
| 32-38 | Student presentations using sentence frames: "x equals...", "I solved by..." | **EN** | Structured output with scaffolds |
| 38-43 | Summary and comprehension check | VN | Verify content understanding |
| 43-45 | Vocabulary recap + exit ticket | **EN** | Reinforce target language |

Source: [Cambridge — Teaching Maths Through English: A CLIL Approach](https://www.cambridgeenglish.org/Images/168751-teaching-maths-through-english-a-clil-approach.pdf)

## Is Mixing Languages Good or Counter-Productive?

### Research supports strategic mixing (translanguaging):
- 82.8% of CLIL learners naturally switch to L1 for difficult topics — a defensive mechanism, not a failure ([ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/327749462_Code-Switching_in_CLIL_the_Students'_Perception))
- Blending translanguaging and CLIL keeps students engaged and enhances both content and language acquisition ([ERIC](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1381333))
- **Risk is not mixing itself, but unstructured random mixing** without pedagogical purpose

### The recommended progression:
- Start low, increase gradually based on readiness
- 50/50 model documented in Spanish higher education ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1475158522000418))
- **Never jump to 100%** without demonstrated teacher and student capacity

## Country Case Studies — Lessons Learned

### Malaysia's PPSMI (2003-2012) — THE WARNING

Mandated Math/Science in English at all levels. **Reversed after 9 years.**

- Only **4% of teachers** used 90%+ English in teaching ([Scientific Malaysian](https://magazine.scientificmalaysian.com/article/opinions/revealing-reality-ppsmi/))
- Student English proficiency improved by only **3%** after 6 years ([Wikipedia - PPSMI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PPSMI))
- **70% of primary students** couldn't comprehend math instruction in English
- **80%** found it difficult to learn math/science in English
- Rural states: 50%+ failure rates in math and science ([ERIC](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1138948.pdf))
- Replaced by MBMMBI policy separating language from content learning

**Key lesson for Vietnam:** Malaysia attempted 100% EMI without teacher prep. The gradual approach (30% → 50% → 80%) is a direct response.

### Rwanda's 2008 English Shift

Mandated English-medium at ALL levels, replacing Kinyarwanda and French.

- **Reduced literacy**, secondary enrollment, and completed schooling ([ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/275122876_Policy_without_a_plan_English_as_a_medium_of_instruction_in_Rwanda))
- By 2011, **8/10 primary teachers** had beginner-level English
- Forced to restore Kinyarwanda for lower primary

### Kazakhstan's Trilingual Education (2000s-present)

20+ years of planning Kazakh + Russian + English. **Still not fully implemented.**

- English not used on equal footing despite two decades ([IIER](https://www.iier.org.au/iier30/polatova.pdf))
- Four barriers: simultaneous reforms, inadequate training, no English environment, linguistic distance ([ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/344356860_Understanding_Trilingual_Education_Reform_in_Kazakhstan_Why_Is_It_Stalled))

### European CLIL Results (Mixed)

Systematic review of longitudinal studies ([SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1474904119872426)):
- **Netherlands**: significant gains for grammar and idioms
- **Italy**: significant gains on ALL tested scales (possible Hawthorne effect)
- **Germany**: no significant gains
- **Spain** (primary): no convincing evidence; one study showed negative listening results

**Methodological concern:** CLIL classes tend to be selective — students may be more motivated to begin with.

## How to Measure EMI Performance — Beyond "We Have Content"

### The Assessment Gap
- **77% of EMI teachers** need more guidance on assessment ([Redalyc](https://www.redalyc.org/journal/5702/570272348069/html/))
- 59% don't use specific rubrics
- Teachers can't distinguish content knowledge gaps from language barriers

### Real Metrics (Not Implementation Theater)

| Metric | What It Measures | How |
|--------|-----------------|-----|
| **Actual classroom English use** | Policy vs reality gap | Classroom observation (Malaysia: only 4% used 90%+ English) |
| **Student English proficiency pre/post** | Language gains | CEFR-aligned testing, receptive AND productive skills separately |
| **Content learning in L1** | Whether content suffers | Same assessment given in Vietnamese as control |
| **Teacher confidence scores** | Readiness trajectory | Self-report + observation |
| **English exposure hours** | Dosage | Track actual minutes, not scheduled minutes |
| **Student comprehension checks** | Real-time understanding | Exit tickets in L1 after English segments |

### ROAD-MAPPING Framework (Dafouz & Smit, 2020)

The most comprehensive EMI evaluation framework, 6 dimensions:
- **R**oles of English, **A**cademic **D**isciplines, (language) **M**anagement, **A**gents, **P**ractices/**P**rocesses, **IN**ternationalisation/**G**localisation

Source: [Springer](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-030-23463-8)

## Teacher Pain Points — What They Actually Need

### The core problem: "not knowing what to say"

Teachers are more anxious about **operational language** (classroom management, transitions, instructions) than content vocabulary. They feel prepared for "equation" but not for "please work with your partner" or "time's up, let's review." ([Macmillan](https://www.macmillanenglish.com/ir/blog-resources/article/emi-english-as-a-medium-of-instruction))

### Vietnamese CLIL teachers specifically

Teachers were placed in CLIL classes **without preparation** and struggle to identify as language-and-content-integrated teachers. ([Taylor & Francis](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09500782.2023.2260374))

### What they need: bite-sized, immediately applicable

- Microlearning is perceived as flexible and stress-free ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10639-023-11964-6))
- All CLIL PD described as "ad hoc and accidental" — teachers lack structured pathways ([ERIC](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1364876.pdf))
- Scaffolding research shows students participate more when teachers have the **process language** ready: "It's your turn," "This goes with this," "We need to put this here" ([Taylor & Francis](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09571736.2019.1705879))

### Two types of language teachers need:

| Type | Examples | Priority |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Academic/Content** | equation, variable, hypothesis, photosynthesis | Teachers already feel somewhat prepared |
| **Operational/Management** | "Open your books to page...", "Work in pairs", "Who can explain?", "Time's up", "Well done", "Let me repeat that" | **This is the gap** — teachers feel unprepared |

## Pronunciation — What Matters

### Intelligibility, not native-like accent

Jenkins' Lingua Franca Core (LFC) identifies what subject teachers MUST get right vs. what doesn't matter:

**Essential (affects intelligibility):**
- Most consonant sounds (except /th/ — substitutions OK)
- Aspiration of initial /p/, /t/, /k/
- Vowel length contrasts ("heat" vs "hit")
- Nuclear/contrastive stress (emphasis on the important word)

**Not essential (doesn't affect intelligibility):**
- Perfect /th/ sounds
- Dark /l/
- Weak forms of function words
- Connected speech features
- Native-like intonation patterns
- Stress-timed rhythm

Source: [ELF Pronunciation](https://elfpron.wordpress.com/2013/11/21/what-is-the-lfc/) | [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/320880895_Pronunciation_for_CLILEMI_Teachers_Insights_from_English_as_a_Lingua_Franca_Research)

### Tools for subject-specific pronunciation:
- **ELSA Speak**: AI pronunciation coach with specific feedback ([ReadSpeaker](https://www.readspeaker.com/blog/why-pronunciation-accuracy-assistive-technology-is-essential/))
- **YouGlish**: Real videos with specific words in authentic contexts
- **Text-to-Speech**: For technical/specialized vocabulary
